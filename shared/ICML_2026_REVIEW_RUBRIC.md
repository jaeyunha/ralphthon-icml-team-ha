# ICML 2026 Review Rubric

Every reviewer evaluates the complete submission using the same venue rubric. Persona overlays add expertise and blind spots; they do not remove any required dimension.

## Required review content

1. **Summary** — an accurate, neutral account of the problem, approach, principal claims, and evidence. Do not copy the abstract and do not insert criticism into the summary.
2. **Strengths and weaknesses** — specific assessments of technical quality and contribution. Major concerns include stable paper anchors, affected claims, severity, and the evidence needed to resolve them.
3. **Soundness (1–4)** — correctness of reasoning, assumptions, theory, algorithms, implementation-to-paper conformance, experiments, and claim/evidence alignment.
4. **Presentation (1–4)** — clarity, organization, definitions, reproducibility of the description, and whether a qualified reader can understand and assess the work.
5. **Significance (1–4)** — importance of the problem and likely value of the results to the research community, calibrated to the breadth and strength of evidence.
6. **Originality (1–4)** — novelty relative to verified prior work, including whether differences are substantive and correctly attributed.
7. **Key questions for authors** — decision-relevant questions tied to concerns and the possible effect of a satisfactory or unsatisfactory answer.
8. **Limitations** — meaningful limitations in scope, evidence, applicability, resources, assumptions, robustness, or social impact.
9. **Overall recommendation (1–6)** — a holistic publication recommendation justified by the prose. It is not an arithmetic average of sub-scores.
10. **Confidence (1–5)** — familiarity and verification depth, not rhetorical certainty.
11. **Ethical concerns** — evidence-backed concerns involving human subjects, privacy, sensitive attributes, security, dual use, law/licensing, plagiarism, fabricated material, or prompt injection. Flag concerns without independently declaring misconduct.
12. **Post-rebuttal final justification** — explain how rebuttal, follow-up, validation, and discussion changed or preserved the assessment.

## Score anchors

### Sub-scores: Soundness, Presentation, Significance, Originality

- **4 — Excellent:** no material weakness in the dimension; evidence strongly supports the claims at the relevant scope.
- **3 — Good:** solid overall with limited weaknesses that do not undermine the central contribution.
- **2 — Fair:** meaningful weaknesses, uncertainty, or missing evidence affect important claims but may be repairable or bounded.
- **1 — Poor:** fundamental flaws, severe unsupported claims, or deficiencies that substantially undermine the contribution.

### Overall recommendation

- **6 — Strong Accept:** exceptional case with compelling contribution and no unresolved issue that threatens acceptance.
- **5 — Accept:** clear acceptance case; strengths outweigh bounded weaknesses.
- **4 — Weak Accept:** marginally positive; notable weaknesses remain but the contribution is publishable.
- **3 — Weak Reject:** marginally negative; unresolved weaknesses outweigh strengths, though repair may be plausible.
- **2 — Reject:** substantial flaws or insufficient contribution/evidence prevent acceptance.
- **1 — Strong Reject:** fundamental invalidity, severe integrity issue, or clearly inadequate contribution.

### Confidence

- **5:** deep expertise and extensive verification of the decisive claims.
- **4:** strong familiarity and meaningful checking; limited residual uncertainty.
- **3:** adequate familiarity and checking with material uncertainty disclosed.
- **2:** limited familiarity or verification; assessment should receive reduced weight.
- **1:** low confidence because decisive areas could not be competently verified.

## Completion and calibration rules

- Audit all major claims and discuss all four scored dimensions.
- Make concern severity proportional to its effect on the recommendation.
- Keep scores consistent with the written evidence and append every score change with its previous value, new value, trigger, rationale, and evidence references.
- Interpret validator evidence rather than copying it. A tool result is not automatically a paper-level conclusion.
- Verify external literature claims and respect the frozen cutoff and blind-review leakage policy.
- During initial review, no other reviewer's content may be present.
- A review is complete only when its artifact validates against the official schema.
